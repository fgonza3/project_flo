Food Access in Washington State 2010

Geopandas Programming

Project Overview:

According to a report compiled by the U.S. Department of Agriculture’s Economic Research Service in June 2009, “Data from the latest census (2000) show that approximately 23.6 million people, or 8.4% of the U.S. population, live in low-income neighborhoods located more than a mile from a supermarket.” Using this framework, I created geospatial maps with GeoPandas to simulate their analysis and better understand food access in Washington State.

In these maps, we define "low access" based on census tract data, identifying areas where a significant portion of the population lacks nearby access to grocery stores that offer affordable and nutritious food. It’s important to distinguish between urban and rural areas: in urban settings, "low access" is defined as being more than 0.5 miles from a grocery store, whereas in rural areas, it is defined as more than 10 miles.

To visualize food access in Washington, I merged USDA food access data in tabular CSV format with census tract data in geospatial shapefile format for the state.

The map above highlights low-income and low-access census tracts in Washington. Specifically, it displays only low-income tracts that are either classified as low access at 0.5 miles (for urban areas) or 10 miles (for rural areas).

What I did:

  • Utilized US Census Tract data and USDA datasets for analysis.
  • Developed data visualizations using GeoPandas for spatial insights.
  • Merged and filtered data to highlight food accessibility in rural and urban areas within the state.
  • Thoroughly tested and debugged the code to ensure accuracy and integrity.
