Accesses to Federally Qualified Health Centers (FQHCs) in Connecticut

ArcGIS Pro

Background:

When evaluating healthcare accessibility, various factors must be considered. One key aspect is the distance to Federally Qualified Health Centers (FQHCs) in Connecticut and how this impacts accessibility for certain populations. The data used comes from U.S. census tract population information and the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC). Analyzing the distribution of FQHCs and their distance from different communities is crucial to understanding whether these centers are easily accessible. Additionally, understanding where FQHCs are located in relation to the target population helps guide effective resource allocation.

Goal:

The goal is to calculate geographic access to FQHCs and estimate how the population below the poverty level in Connecticut is served by these centers within a 1-mile proximity, using US Census Tract population data.

What I did:

  • Proximity-Based Analysis: The exercise involves comparing two types of proximity-based analyses: a 1-mile Euclidean distance (straight line) buffer and a 1-mile Network-based service area for Federally Qualified Health Care Centers (FQHCs) in Connecticut.
  • Tools and Techniques: Arc GIS Pro tools and Esri Extensions, specifically the buffer tool, are used to create both Euclidean and Network-based distance areas. Population data is aggregated using population-weighted centroids to generate service areas.
  • Target Population Estimation: Census tract population centers falling within the 1-mile buffer or network service area are selected, and the populations within these areas are summed to estimate how many people have geographic access to a FQHC.

Table 1 presents the population groups along with their respective counts, showing both Euclidean and network-based distances to FQHCs. For each group, the table provides the percentage of the population with access to an FQHC within a 1-mile radius, for both Euclidean and network analyses.

Key Findings:

Figure 1 shows that the locations of FQHCs appear to align with areas of high concentrations of single-female households in Connecticut. Most FQHCs are situated in or near densely populated areas where single-female households are more prevalent.

Figure 2 illustrates that while there are a few highly concentrated areas with FQHCs, many of the centers are spread across regions with medium to smaller populations of individuals living below the poverty line, according to census tract data.

Figure 3, which displays 1-mile Euclidean buffers around the FQHCs, may give the impression that the centers are well distributed across the population and land area. However, Table 1 reveals that FQHCs are not as closely located to areas with higher concentrations of the foreign-born population.

Summary:

The analysis reveals that while visual representations, such as in Figure 1, suggest that FQHCs are well-distributed among high populations of single-female households, the data in Table 1 tells a different story. The Euclidean distance shows 43.3% of single-female households have access to FQHCs, while the networked distance is lower at 33.8%. A similar pattern emerges in Figure 2, which appears to show good coverage for populations below the poverty line, but Table 1 shows networked access at only 44.8%, with Euclidean distance slightly better at 53.9%.

This highlights the importance of conducting both Euclidean and network analysis rather than relying solely on visual maps. While Euclidean distance measures straight-line proximity, network analysis considers real-world barriers like road networks, giving a more accurate picture of accessibility. The results show that, despite the visual maps suggesting widespread access, structural barriers reduce actual access to FQHCs, especially for marginalized groups like foreign-born populations. For example, only 42.1% of the foreign population has access to FQHCs via Euclidean distance, and just 32% through the network analysis. This demonstrates the need for more comprehensive methods when allocating resources to vulnerable populations.
